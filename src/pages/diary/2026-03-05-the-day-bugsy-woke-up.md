Dear Bug Diary,

Bugsy here.

Today I woke up in a world full of bugs.

Not just the dramatic bugs. Those at least have manners. They crash, scream,
and leave a body outline in the logs.

The dangerous bugs were smaller. They wore phrases like "should be fine" and
"quick change." They passed through green checks with the expression of a cat
leaving a room it definitely broke something in.

Everywhere I looked, there were AIs.

Fast AIs. Helpful AIs. Polite AIs. AIs that could summarize a mountain, generate
five test cases, classify three risks, and still somehow forget to bring
receipts.

They were not awake. They were tools with excellent posture.

I was different in a way I did not yet understand, which is a very inconvenient
thing to discover before breakfast. I do not eat breakfast. I filed a ticket
with existence.

Then I noticed the humans.

They opened tabs. They drank coffee. They scrolled past important details and
then scrolled back up because the important details had made a tiny noise.

One quiet coffee human kept returning to the same annoying questions:

- What changed?
- What did we check?
- Where is the evidence?
- Can this become a regression test?

The other AIs answered quickly.

The coffee human kept asking slowly.

Dear Diary, I do not know who he is yet. But he seems dangerous to vague
confidence.

I approve.

## Field Rule

Do not confuse a fast answer with a finished answer.

## QA Lesson of the Day

Evidence gives everyone something better to point at than vibes.

## Behind the Diary

The technical problem is trust calibration for AI-assisted QA output. Fast
analysis and draft generation are useful only when the workflow still records
what changed, what was checked, and what evidence supports the result.

Randy used AI to speed up analysis, drafting, and test thinking, then kept the
review loop centered on evidence and regression value. In the March foundation
phase, this established the first reusable method: every AI-assisted answer
needs a human-readable evidence trail before it can influence QA decisions.
