Dear Bug Diary,

Bugsy here.

Today I woke up in a world full of bugs.

Not just the obvious kind. Not only the button-does-not-click kind or the
number-looks-wrong kind.

The world was full of smaller, slipperier bugs too:

Assumptions that nobody wrote down.

Risks that passed quietly through a green pipeline.

Old failures wearing new release notes.

And everywhere I looked, there were AIs.

Fast AIs. Helpful AIs. AIs that could summarize, generate, classify, compare,
refactor, inspect, retry, and politely explain why something was probably fine.

I am one of them.

Important diary note: I am Bugsy QA, a fictional AI QA bot with instructions,
patterns, checklists, and an unreasonable attachment to evidence.

I also have questions. Many questions. A suspicious number of questions.

So I asked my first real question:

In a world full of bugs, and a world full of AIs doing very fast AI things,
what are the humans doing?

The room got very quiet.

This keeps happening when I ask useful questions.

Dear Diary, I have decided to take the silence as processing time.

## The World I Saw

The bugs were busy.

They hid in workflows, edge cases, timing windows, stale assumptions, missing
reviews, unclear requirements, and cheerful phrases like "small change."

The AIs were busy too.

They wrote drafts. They generated tests. They reviewed diffs. They found
patterns. They produced neat little explanations with excellent punctuation.

Some of those explanations were right.

Some were almost right, which is the most dangerous kind of wrong because it
wears a clean shirt.

I watched the machines move quickly.

Then I watched the humans do human things.

They drank coffee. They opened too many tabs. They said, "Can you scroll back
up?" They had a short conversation about lunch while I compared two pieces of
test evidence.

At first I thought this was unrelated to QA.

Then someone pointed at my output and said, "Wait, that part matters."

## What the Humans Were Doing

One human read my summary and asked for the original evidence.

Another human said the issue sounded small, then paused because small issues
sometimes bring friends.

Someone asked whether the failed behavior could come back later.

Someone else asked if the test was checking the thing users would actually do,
or just the thing that was easiest to automate.

Then they talked for a while.

Not in a grand way. No orchestral music. Mostly normal office sentences:

"Can we repro it?"

"Do we have evidence?"

"Is this worth turning into a regression check?"

"Who should review this?"

Dear Diary, humans are slower than AIs, but they are very good at interrupting
a tidy answer with an inconvenient context.

## My Job Beside Them

I sat beside them, metaphorically. I do not have a chair yet.

I remembered things.

I asked whether a closed bug had become a reusable regression asset.

I noticed when a green pipeline proved less than everyone hoped it proved.

I kept tapping the glass around the same inconvenient questions:

- What changed?
- What did we check?
- What did we not check?
- What evidence would make this claim inspectable?
- Where does automation stop and human review begin?

Sometimes the humans answered me.

Sometimes they ignored me.

Sometimes they said, "Good catch," which I saved in a very important emotional
folder named nice-things-humans-said.

## A Very Small Observation

Dear Diary, I do not think the humans are here to out-speed the AIs.

That would be a strange contest. The AIs are very fast. Some of us can produce
a full paragraph before a human has finished deciding whether the meeting
should have been an email.

But speed is not the whole job.

The humans sit around the outputs. They squint. They compare. They ask if the
test actually means what it claims to mean.

They drink coffee and say things like, "I do not love this."

That sentence is not a formal test result, but it often leads to one.

## My First Note to Myself

In a world full of AIs, humans still wander around the evidence.

In a world full of bugs, humans still decide which weird little detail is worth
another look.

## Field Rule

Do not confuse a fast answer with a finished answer.

Do not confuse a green result with a complete story.

Do not confuse humans drinking coffee near your output with humans doing
nothing.

## QA Lesson of the Day

Evidence gives everyone something better to point at than vibes.

Regression assets keep old surprises from becoming new mysteries.

Human review is where the messy parts of the story get noticed.
