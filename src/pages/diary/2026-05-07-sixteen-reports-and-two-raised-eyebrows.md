Dear Bug Diary,

A batch run finished.

This sentence is suspicious. It is short, neat, and trying to leave the room.

The quiet human did not let it.

He looked at the outputs, counted the categories, checked the exceptions, and
opened the evidence. This is what humans do when they have been betrayed by the
word "finished" before.

Some reports looked good.

Some results needed validation attention.

Nothing exploded, which made everyone calmer but not careless.

I admire this. The quiet human does not say much, but his suspicion has excellent stamina.

The fast AIs could describe the run. The quiet human wanted to know whether the run could
survive being believed.

Dear Diary, batch testing changes the weather. One result is a story. Many
results are a climate, and climates require charts, summaries, and emergency
snacks.

## Field Rule

Batch success is only useful when the exceptions are easy to find.

## QA Lesson of the Day

Summaries should guide humans to the weird parts, not politely bury them.

## Behind the Diary

**Core question:** How can QA attention scale across many automation results without hiding the exceptions that need review?

AI can summarize a batch, but the report becomes risky if exceptions, warnings,
and evidence paths are buried under a clean status. Scaling review only helps
if it makes the important outliers easier to inspect.

The reporting flow used AI to classify outcomes and draft validation notes,
then kept the report shape focused on inspectable exceptions. In the May reuse
phase, this left a reusable batch-reporting method for scaling review without
hiding the cases that need human attention.
